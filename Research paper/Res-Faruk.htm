Factors Influencing Birth Registration in Bangladesh – A

Case Study in Sunamganj Pourashava

 

M. Faruk Hossain

Research Student, Department of Statistics, Shahjalal University of Science & Technology, Sylhet-3114, Bangladesh

 

Abstract

 

   The government of Bangladesh ratified the UN Convention on the Rights of the Children (CRC) in 1990, in which birth registration is considered as one of the fundamental rights of the children. Birth registration is the official recording of a child by the local government unit. It is a  system of recording the incidence of a birth and keeping the record for administrative purposes. Birth registration has two main objectives: legal and statistical. Ideally, it is a part of an effective civil registration system that legally acknowledges the existence of the person with identity, establishes the child’s family ties and recording of major life events from birth to death. Civil registration provides the demographic data which allow a country to keep track of its population’s statistics, trends and differentials. Every government needs to know how many people are in the country and how it can plan to meet their need.

 

   This paper aims to assess the birth registration activities currently being carried out by Sunamganj pourashava as well as identify the factors influencing birth registration at household level. The outcome of this study will help to formulate recommendation for an effective birth registration system in other pourashava of Bangladesh.

 

   Data for this study was collected from two types of respondents—parents at the household level and key informants. Households were selected using cluster sampling technique in which a mahalla was considered as a cluster. Clusters were chosen based on systematic probability proportionate to size sampling procedure. A total of 900 households covering 10 clusters were selected and on an average 90 households from each cluster were chosen as a compact segment. In addition, a total of 22 key informants representing different levels in the pourashava were chosen. Data were collected during May-July, 2005.

 

   The status of birth registration in Sunamgonj Pourashava was not found satisfactory—only 265 children (43.3 percent) was found registered and in 80 cases of these registrations parents had no idea about the registration. Such cases were found as registered after checking the register book by matching their date of birth, name and parentage identity collected during household interview. Only 22 percent registrations were done voluntarily and 35 percent upon the persuasion of pourashava health workers. The findings show that about half of the parents had no idea of birth registration and those who had some idea, gathered information mainly from television programs and pourashava campaigns.

 

   The study of differentials of birth registration shows that the children of lower and higher ages, born in clinic/hospital, and who has given all the vaccinations observed a higher registration rate. Gender disparity has not been observed among the children with respect to birth registration. Further, the birth registration was observed higher for the children whose parents were more educated, engaged in business and service, more exposed to the media, with some pervious knowledge of birth registration. Birth registration increased with the increased level of household income and higher standard of living. The results from multivariate logistic regression analysis suggest that father’s education,  parents previous knowledge of birth registration, age of child and household income have had significant effect on birth registration Bangladesh. The chance of birth registration was found to be increasing the increasing level of father’s education as well as household income. The youngest child and whose parents had some previous knowledge of birth registration has been observed more likely to be registered.

   The study indicated that  age of child, eduation of father, parents previous knowledge on birth registration and household income­­­- all have significant influence on birth registration. Therefore, these factors may be considered for motivating people for birth registration. Special arrangement to reach out to parents with no or low education, low economic status, and limited exposure to the media may have a positive effect on birth registration.

 

   The study makes some recommendations for policy implications by considering the findings as well as by reviewing strategies adopted by GOB. An effective way to increase birth registration would be to develop awareness. Special awareness programmes may be undertaken in order to educate parents on birth registration with particular emphasis on the proper execution of the updated ‘birth and death law 2004’.
